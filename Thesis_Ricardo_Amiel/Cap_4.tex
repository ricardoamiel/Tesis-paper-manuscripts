\chapter{Low Rank Building Change Detection Method}
\label{chap:method}

This chapter presents the complete engineering design and mathematical
formalization of the proposed RALA-ChangeFormer framework for bi-temporal
building change detection from very high-resolution (VHR) satellite imagery.
The framework addresses a dual challenge: achieving representational fidelity
comparable to full quadratic Transformer architectures while reducing the
$\mathcal{O}(N^2)$ memory cost of standard multi-head self-attention to a
regime that is feasible on research-grade GPU hardware. The solution pursued
here integrates the rank-augmented linear attention mechanism of
\cite{Fan2025RALA} as a drop-in encoder substitution within the hierarchical
Siamese architecture of ChangeFormer \cite{Bandara2022ChangeFormer}, preserving
the full multi-scale encoder-decoder topology and loss supervision structure
of the baseline while replacing its computational bottleneck with a provably
rank-preserving linear alternative.

The overall workflow of the proposed method is organized into three operational
stages, illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:method-flow}: a preprocessing stage
that standardizes the bi-temporal input pairs, a detection stage in which the
RALA-enhanced encoder-decoder architecture extracts, compares, and decodes
bi-temporal representations into a dense probability map, and a localization
stage in which the probability map is thresholded and morphologically refined
into a binary change mask suitable for urban analysis and evaluation. The
remainder of this chapter develops each stage in formal detail.
Section~\ref{sec:problem-notation} establishes the mathematical problem
formulation. Section~\ref{sec:preprocessing} describes the preprocessing
protocol. Section~\ref{sec:detection} presents the core RALA-ChangeFormer
architecture, encompassing the patch embedding and tokenization design, the
principled justification for the encoder-level RALA substitution, the
complete multi-temporal feature reweighting equations, and the joint loss
function. Section~\ref{sec:localization} details the localization stage.
Section~\ref{sec:final-considerations} synthesizes the methodological
contributions and their implications for scalable VHR urban monitoring.

\section{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:problem-notation}
% =============================================================================

The building change detection task is formalized as a dense binary prediction
problem over co-registered bi-temporal satellite image pairs. Let
$I_{t_1}, I_{t_2} \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times C}$ denote two images of
the same geographic area acquired at epochs $t_1 < t_2$, where $H$, $W$, and
$C$ denote spatial height, width, and number of spectral channels. The goal
is to learn a parametric mapping
\begin{equation}
f_\theta : (I_{t_1},\, I_{t_2}) \;\longmapsto\; \hat{Y} \in [0,1]^{H \times W},
\label{eq:problem_mapping}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{Y}_{ij}$ is the model's estimated probability that a semantically
meaningful structural building change occurred at pixel $(i,j)$ between $t_1$
and $t_2$. The ground-truth reference is a binary mask
$Y \in \{0,1\}^{H \times W}$, where $Y_{ij}=1$ indicates genuine change
(construction, demolition, or substantial structural modification of a building)
and $Y_{ij}=0$ indicates absence of change.

The learning objective minimizes a hybrid loss $\mathcal{L}$ over a labelled
training set $\mathcal{T} = \{(I_{t_1}^{(n)}, I_{t_2}^{(n)}, Y^{(n)})\}_{n=1}^{M}$:
\begin{equation}
\min_{\theta}\;\mathcal{L}\bigl(Y,\,\hat{Y}\bigr)
    = \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{BCE}}\bigl(Y,\,\hat{Y}\bigr)
    + \lambda\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{Dice}}\bigl(Y,\,\hat{Y}\bigr),
\label{eq:training_objective}
\end{equation}
where the Binary Cross-Entropy term $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{BCE}}$ enforces
pixel-wise predictive calibration and the soft Dice term
$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{Dice}}$ enforces overlap-based consistency between
the predicted and ground-truth change regions. The scalar $\lambda \geq 0$
controls the relative weight of the two terms and is set to $\lambda = 1.0$
throughout the experiments of Chapter~\ref{chap:experiments}. At inference
time, the continuous probability map $\hat{Y}$ is converted to a binary mask
via a fixed threshold $\tau = 0.5$:
\begin{equation}
Y_{\mathrm{bin}}(i,j) =
\begin{cases}
1, & \hat{Y}(i,j) \geq \tau, \\
0, & \text{otherwise.}
\end{cases}
\label{eq:threshold}
\end{equation}
This formulation treats BCD as a pixel-level binary segmentation task, which
is consistent with the dominant approach in the recent literature
\cite{Bandara2022ChangeFormer, Fang2021SNUNetCD, Zhang2020FusionNetDSIFN}
and enables evaluation with standard dense prediction metrics including $F_1$
score and Intersection over Union (IoU), as defined in
Section~\ref{sec:metrics}.

\section{Data Preprocessing}
\label{sec:preprocessing}
% =============================================================================

Bi-temporal satellite images require consistent spatial and radiometric
alignment before they can be processed by any deep model. Misalignment
between temporal acquisitions, whether geometric or radiometric in origin,
introduces systematic false change signals that are structurally
indistinguishable from genuine construction events and therefore degrade
both training and inference quality \cite{Hussain2013Review}. The
preprocessing stage applied to all datasets used in this thesis addresses
these failure modes through three sequential operations.

\paragraph{Co-registration and geometric normalization.}
Both images $I_{t_1}$ and $I_{t_2}$ are aligned to a common spatial
reference frame through affine geometric corrections. Residual misalignment
is further reduced via cross-correlation refinement, which estimates
sub-pixel displacement fields between temporally overlapping structures and
applies a spatially smooth correction. This step is critical for datasets
with long temporal baselines, where satellite orbit and attitude variations
can produce positional offsets that would otherwise produce a systematic
false-change perimeter around every stable building boundary.

\paragraph{Radiometric normalization.}
All spectral channels are normalized to the unit interval by a per-image
min-max rescaling:
\begin{equation}
I' = \frac{I - \min(I)}{\max(I) - \min(I) + \varepsilon},
\label{eq:normalization}
\end{equation}
where $\varepsilon > 0$ is a small constant for numerical stability. This
normalization removes absolute radiometric offsets caused by sensor gain
differences and atmospheric illumination variation between acquisition dates,
ensuring that the model's feature extraction is driven by relative spectral
contrasts within each image rather than by absolute intensity levels.

\paragraph{Patch extraction and augmentation.}
Following the training protocol established by \cite{Bandara2022ChangeFormer},
each co-registered and normalized bi-temporal pair is divided into
non-overlapping $256 \times 256$-pixel patches. Each patch yields a
bi-temporal tensor of dimension $(256, 256, 2C)$, which constitutes a single
training sample. This strategy both expands the effective training set size
and constrains the per-sample GPU memory footprint to a range compatible with
standard research hardware. During training, random horizontal and vertical
flips, random $90°$ rotations, and mild brightness and contrast jitter are
applied stochastically to each patch, improving model generalization to the
radiometric diversity encountered across the six benchmark datasets.

\section{The RALA-ChangeFormer Architecture}
\label{sec:detection}
% =============================================================================

\subsection{Architectural Overview}

RALA-ChangeFormer is a hierarchical Siamese encoder-decoder architecture
for dense bi-temporal change detection. Its design inherits the full structural
topology of ChangeFormer \cite{Bandara2022ChangeFormer}---a shared
convolutional-Transformer encoder, a multi-scale feature difference module,
and a lightweight MLP decoder---with the single modification that every
Multi-Head Self-Attention (MHSA) block in the encoder is replaced by a
Rank-Augmented Linear Attention (RALA) block \cite{Fan2025RALA}. All other
components, including the patch embedding modules, layer normalization, feed-forward
networks (FFN), skip connections, and the decoder head, remain identical to
the baseline. This drop-in substitution design preserves full architectural
compatibility with the pretrained SegFormer-B2 encoder weights, enabling
initialization from ImageNet pretraining without any modification to the
loading procedure.

The complete architecture is illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:changeformer-arch}.
Given a bi-temporal input pair $(I_{t_1}, I_{t_2}) \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times C}$,
the two images are independently fed into a weight-sharing encoder. The
encoder operates at four hierarchical resolution levels $l = 1, 2, 3, 4$,
progressively reducing spatial resolution by factors of $\{4, 8, 16, 32\}$
relative to the input while increasing channel dimensionality. At each level,
the corresponding bi-temporal feature maps $F_{t_1}^{(l)}$ and $F_{t_2}^{(l)}$
are extracted. Their element-wise difference $\Delta^{(l)} = F_{t_1}^{(l)} - F_{t_2}^{(l)}$
is computed at each scale and passed through the decoder, which upsamples and
fuses the multi-scale difference representations into the final binary change
probability map $\hat{Y} \in [0,1]^{H \times W}$.

    \centering

    \caption{Overview of the RALA-ChangeFormer architecture. Two input images
    are processed by a weight-sharing hierarchical Siamese encoder that
    produces multi-scale feature maps at four resolution levels. At each
    scale, the bi-temporal features are concatenated, projected into a token
    sequence, and processed by a RALA Transformer block. The multi-scale

\end{figure}

\subsection{Bi-Temporal Patch Embedding and Tokenization}
\label{subsec:patch-embedding}

At each encoder level $l$, the two temporal feature maps
$F_{t_1}^{(l)}, F_{t_2}^{(l)} \in \mathbb{R}^{H_l \times W_l \times C_l}$---
where $H_l = H/2^{l+1}$, $W_l = W/2^{l+1}$, and $C_l$ increases with
depth---are concatenated channel-wise to form the joint bi-temporal
representation:
\begin{equation}
G^{(l)} = \bigl[F_{t_1}^{(l)} \;\Vert\; F_{t_2}^{(l)}\bigr]
          \in \mathbb{R}^{H_l \times W_l \times 2C_l}.
\label{eq:concatenation}
\end{equation}
A lightweight linear patch embedding module then projects $G^{(l)}$ into a
flat token sequence by applying a spatial reshaping and a learned linear
projection of dimension $C_l$:
\begin{equation}
X^{(l)} = \mathrm{PatchEmbed}\!\bigl(G^{(l)}\bigr)
         \in \mathbb{R}^{N_l \times C_l},
\quad N_l = H_l W_l,
\label{eq:patch_embed}
\end{equation}
where $N_l$ is the number of spatial tokens at level $l$. Each row
$X_i^{(l)} \in \mathbb{R}^{C_l}$ encodes the fused bi-temporal information
at spatial position $i$, and the sequence $X^{(l)}$ is the input consumed by
the RALA Transformer block at that encoder level.

The sequence length $N_l$ is the primary driver of computational cost in the
attention operation: under standard quadratic MHSA, the memory footprint scales
as $\mathcal{O}(N_l^2)$, which for a $256 \times 256$ input patch reaches
$N_l = 4096$ at the finest encoder level and demands the construction of a
$4096 \times 4096$ attention matrix per head. This quadratic scaling is the
architectural bottleneck that RALA is designed to eliminate by replacing the
dense matrix computation with a $C_l \times C_l$ key-value buffer whose size
is independent of $N_l$.

\subsection{Encoder-Level Drop-In RALA Substitution}
\label{subsec:rala-substitution}

The architectural justification for replacing MHSA exclusively within the
encoder stages, rather than in both encoder and decoder, rests on a
computational profiling argument. In ChangeFormer, the encoder processes the
largest token sequences encountered anywhere in the network---$N_l$ ranges
from $N_1 = H_1 W_1$ at the finest level to $N_4 = H_4 W_4$ at the coarsest---
while the decoder operates on progressively upsampled intermediate
representations that are substantially smaller. The attention-related memory
cost is therefore dominated by the encoder, and substituting RALA at this
stage captures the vast majority of the efficiency gain while leaving the
decoder architecture, its skip connection logic, and the upstream FFN layers
completely unchanged.

The RALA block is a structural drop-in replacement: it accepts the same
token sequence $X^{(l)} \in \mathbb{R}^{N_l \times C_l}$ as input and
produces an output of the same shape, using an identical residual connection
and layer normalization wrapper. Formally, let $A^{(l)}$ denote the attention
output at encoder level $l$. The block output is:
\begin{equation}
X_{\mathrm{out}}^{(l)} = X^{(l)} + \mathrm{LN}\bigl(A^{(l)}\bigr),
\qquad
Y^{(l)} = \mathrm{FFN}\!\bigl(X_{\mathrm{out}}^{(l)}\bigr),
\label{eq:residual_ffn}
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{LN}(\cdot)$ is layer normalization and $\mathrm{FFN}(\cdot)$
is a two-layer feed-forward network with GELU activation. In the baseline ChangeFormer configuration, $A^{(l)} = \mathrm{MHSA}(X^{(l)})$; conversely, inside the proposed \ac{RALA-CF} network, $A^{(l)} = \mathrm{RALA}(X^{(l)})$. Every other component of the block: the normalization, FFN, skip connection, and downstream fusion is shared identically between the two variants.

\subsection{Mathematical Multi-Temporal Feature Reweighting}
\label{subsec:rala-math}

Within each RALA block at encoder level $l$, the input token sequence
$X^{(l)} \in \mathbb{R}^{N_l \times C_l}$ is first projected into query,
key, and value representations via learned linear projections
$W_Q, W_K, W_V \in \mathbb{R}^{C_l \times d}$:
\begin{equation}
Q = X^{(l)} W_Q, \qquad K = X^{(l)} W_K, \qquad V = X^{(l)} W_V,
\quad Q, K, V \in \mathbb{R}^{N_l \times d}.
\label{eq:qkv_proj}
\end{equation}
A positive-definite kernel feature map $\kappa: \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}^d$
is then applied row-wise to $Q$ and $K$, enabling the key-value product to
be precomputed as a fixed-size global buffer independently of $N_l$.

\paragraph{Global query descriptor.}
To enable token-relevance-aware buffer accumulation, RALA introduces a global
query descriptor $Q_g \in \mathbb{R}^{1 \times d}$ computed as the mean of
all query vectors:
\begin{equation}
Q_g = \frac{1}{N_l} \sum_{i=1}^{N_l} Q_i.
\label{eq:global_query}
\end{equation}
The descriptor $Q_g$ encodes the average semantic query intent of the entire
token sequence at level $l$, providing a global reference against which the
informativeness of individual key vectors is assessed.

\paragraph{Token relevance weights.}
Using $Q_g$ as a probe, a scalar relevance weight $\alpha_j \in \mathbb{R}_{>0}$
is assigned to each token $j$ via a softmax normalization:
\begin{equation}
\alpha_j = N_l \times
    \frac{\exp\!\bigl(Q_g\,\kappa(K_j)^\top\bigr)}
    {\displaystyle\sum_{m=1}^{N_l} \exp\!\bigl(Q_g\,\kappa(K_m)^\top\bigr)},
\label{eq:alpha_weights}
\end{equation}
where the factor $N_l$ preserves the expected magnitude of the subsequent
buffer accumulation. Tokens whose key $K_j$ is semantically aligned with the
global query receive disproportionately large $\alpha_j$, amplifying their
contribution to the global key-value buffer.

\paragraph{Rank-augmented key-value buffer.}
The global key-value buffer $B \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$ is assembled as
the $\alpha$-weighted sum of outer products:
\begin{equation}
B = \sum_{j=1}^{N_l} \alpha_j\, \kappa(K_j)^\top V_j.
\label{eq:rala_buffer}
\end{equation}
Because the weights $\alpha_j$ break the uniform aggregation of standard
linear attention, the effective diversity of the outer products
$\kappa(K_j)^\top V_j$ contributing to $B$ is increased, raising
$\mathrm{rank}(B)$ above the collapsed rank observed under uniform
accumulation. The buffer $B$ is computed once per attention block at cost
$\mathcal{O}(N_l d^2)$ and is independent of $N_l$ in memory.

\paragraph{Output feature modulation.}
A preliminary per-token attention output $Z_i \in \mathbb{R}^d$ is obtained by
querying the buffer:
\begin{equation}
Z_i = \kappa(Q_i)\, B.
\label{eq:Z_pre_mod}
\end{equation}
However, the rank inequality $\mathrm{rank}(Z) \leq \min(\mathrm{rank}(\kappa(Q)),\,
\mathrm{rank}(B)) \leq d$ establishes that $Z$ may still be low-rank when the
query matrix $\kappa(Q)$ is structurally low-rank---a common condition for
spatially correlated remote sensing tokens. To break this constraint, RALA
applies a token-wise Hadamard modulation:
\begin{equation}
Y_i = \phi(X_i^{(l)}) \odot Z_i = \phi(X_i^{(l)}) \odot \bigl(\kappa(Q_i)\,B\bigr),
\label{eq:hadamard_mod}
\end{equation}
where $\phi: \mathbb{R}^{C_l} \to \mathbb{R}^d$ is a learned per-token
transformation (implemented as a lightweight gated linear unit operating
channel-wise) and $\odot$ denotes the Hadamard (element-wise) product.
Because $\phi(X_i^{(l)})$ is distinct for each token $i$ by construction,
the modulated output matrix
$Y = [\phi(X_1^{(l)}) \odot Z_1, \ldots, \phi(X_{N_l}^{(l)}) \odot Z_{N_l}]^\top$
can achieve rank up to $d$ independently of the rank of $\kappa(Q)$, fully
restoring output feature diversity. The Hadamard product and $\phi(\cdot)$
operate channel-wise without any $N_l \times N_l$ pairwise computation,
preserving the $\mathcal{O}(N_l d^2)$ total complexity of the RALA block.

\subsection{Segmentation Decoder and Joint Loss Function}
\label{subsec:decoder-loss}

\paragraph{Multi-scale MLP decoder.}
The decoder receives the set of multi-scale feature difference maps
$\{\Delta^{(l)}\}_{l=1}^{4}$, where each
$\Delta^{(l)} = F_{t_1}^{(l)} - F_{t_2}^{(l)} \in \mathbb{R}^{H_l \times W_l \times C_l}$
encodes the element-wise discrepancy between the bi-temporal encoder outputs
at resolution level $l$. Following the design of \cite{Bandara2022ChangeFormer},
a lightweight All-MLP decoder is applied: each $\Delta^{(l)}$ is first passed
through a linear projection to a common channel dimension $C_{\mathrm{dec}}$,
then bilinearly upsampled to the finest encoder resolution $H_1 \times W_1$,
and the four upsampled representations are concatenated and projected by a
final linear layer to a single-channel logit map. A sigmoid activation converts
the logit map to the probability map $\hat{Y} \in [0,1]^{H \times W}$ at full
input resolution through a terminal bilinear upsampling.

\paragraph{Binary Cross-Entropy loss.}
The first component of the joint loss is pixel-wise Binary Cross-Entropy,
which penalizes predictive miscalibration at each spatial position
independently:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{BCE}}(Y, \hat{Y})
    = -\frac{1}{HW} \sum_{i,j}
      \Bigl[
        Y_{ij} \log \hat{Y}_{ij}
        + (1 - Y_{ij}) \log(1 - \hat{Y}_{ij})
      \Bigr].
\label{eq:bce_loss}
\end{equation}
Because changed pixels constitute only 1--10 percent of the total pixel count
in typical VHR BCD datasets, $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{BCE}}$ alone tends to
drive the model toward the no-change prediction, which achieves low cross-entropy
despite having zero recall on the change class. The soft Dice loss is included
precisely to counteract this tendency.

\paragraph{Soft Dice loss.}
The soft Dice loss directly optimizes the overlap coefficient between the
predicted probability map and the ground-truth mask:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{Dice}}(Y, \hat{Y})
    = 1 - \frac{2\displaystyle\sum_{i,j} Y_{ij}\,\hat{Y}_{ij}}
               {\displaystyle\sum_{i,j} Y_{ij}
                + \displaystyle\sum_{i,j} \hat{Y}_{ij} + \varepsilon},
\label{eq:dice_loss}
\end{equation}
where $\varepsilon > 0$ prevents division by zero on zero-change images.
The numerator is proportional to the pixel-level true positive count, and
the denominator approximates the sum of predicted and ground-truth changed
pixel counts. Minimizing $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{Dice}}$ directly maximizes
the F1 score on the change class, providing a complementary training signal
to the pixel-wise BCE term.

\paragraph{Joint training objective.}
The two terms are combined with equal weight into the joint optimization
objective:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}(Y, \hat{Y})
    = \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{BCE}}(Y, \hat{Y})
    + \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{Dice}}(Y, \hat{Y}).
\label{eq:joint_loss}
\end{equation}
Parameters $\theta$ are optimized using AdamW with an initial learning rate of
$1 \times 10^{-4}$ and a cosine annealing schedule. For LEVIR-CD, training
is initialized from SegFormer-B2 weights pretrained on ADE20K, following the
protocol of \cite{Bandara2022ChangeFormer}. For DSIFN-CD and the four extended
benchmarks, the model is fine-tuned from the best LEVIR-CD checkpoint using a
reduced learning rate of $6 \times 10^{-5}$, enabling efficient transfer of
the learned multi-scale bi-temporal representations.

\section{Change Localization}
\label{sec:localization}
% =============================================================================

The localization stage converts the continuous probability map
$\hat{Y} \in [0,1]^{H \times W}$ produced by the RALA-ChangeFormer decoder
into an interpretable binary change mask through a deterministic
post-processing pipeline. This stage is computationally lightweight and
parameter-free: it operates exclusively through fixed operations and does not
require any additional learning.

\paragraph{Binary mask generation.}
A hard thresholding operation is applied to the probability map, converting
it into the discrete binary mask $M \in \{0,1\}^{H \times W}$:
\begin{equation}
M(i,j) =
\begin{cases}
1, & \hat{Y}(i,j) \geq \tau, \\
0, & \text{otherwise,}
\end{cases}
\label{eq:masking}
\end{equation}
where $\tau = 0.5$ is the detection threshold. This value is consistent with
prior work in the change detection literature \cite{Bandara2022ChangeFormer,
Zheng2022HFANet} and provides a balanced precision-recall operating point for
typical urban VHR datasets. Pixels with $M(i,j) = 1$ are classified as
changed buildings; all remaining pixels are classified as unchanged.

\paragraph{Morphological refinement.}
Despite the spatial coherence of the learned representations, the raw binary
mask may contain isolated false-positive pixels or fragmented change regions
near building edges, particularly in scenes with strong shadow gradients or
misregistration residuals. A morphological post-processing sequence is applied
to address these artifacts: erosion suppresses isolated noise pixels by removing
foreground components that do not satisfy a minimum spatial extent criterion;
dilation restores the spatial continuity of genuine change regions that may
have been partially suppressed by erosion; and a closing operation---dilation
followed by erosion---consolidates fragmented change boundaries into
geometrically coherent contiguous regions. These operations improve the visual
and quantitative quality of the final mask without introducing any
geometry-specific assumptions that would limit generalization across datasets.

\paragraph{Multi-scale reconstruction and visualization.}
The refined binary mask $M$, initially computed at the $256 \times 256$ patch
resolution, is bilinearly upsampled to the native spatial resolution of the
input imagery---$1024 \times 1024$ pixels for LEVIR-CD and $512 \times 512$
pixels for DSIFN-CD---to enable direct overlay visualization on the original
pre-event and post-event images. This visualization facilitates qualitative
evaluation of the spatial precision with which the model delineates building
boundaries and supports their use in downstream urban planning and damage
assessment workflows.

\section{Final Considerations}
\label{sec:final-considerations}
% =============================================================================

This chapter has presented the complete design of RALA-ChangeFormer: a hierarchical Siamese Transformer architecture for bi-temporal building change detection. Within this framework, the quadratic self-attention bottleneck originally identified in the baseline ChangeFormer model by \cite{Bandara2022ChangeFormer} has been systematically replaced
by the rank-augmented linear attention mechanism of \cite{Fan2025RALA} at
every encoder stage. The presentation has proceeded from the formal problem
specification through the preprocessing protocol, the architectural design and
mathematical derivation of the RALA substitution, the decoder structure and
joint loss function, and the deterministic localization pipeline.

The central engineering contribution documented in this chapter is the
encoder-level RALA substitution and its theoretical justification. The
substitution is motivated by three properties: first, the $\mathcal{O}(N_l d^2)$
versus $\mathcal{O}(N_l^2 d)$ asymptotic advantage of RALA over MHSA,
which is decisive for VHR inputs where $N_l \gg d$; second, the structural
drop-in compatibility of the RALA block with every other component of the
ChangeFormer architecture, enabling initialization from pretrained encoder
weights without any re-engineering of the decoder, skip connections, or FFN
layers; and third, the formal guarantee, established in
Section~\ref{sec:rala-theory}, that RALA's global query reweighting and
Hadamard output modulation jointly prevent the rank collapse that afflicts
standard linear attention in dense prediction settings.

The joint BCE and soft Dice loss function formalizes the training objective
for the class-imbalanced BCD setting, where changed pixels constitute a
small minority of the total pixel count. The BCE term ensures pixel-wise
calibration while the Dice term directly optimizes the F1 overlap coefficient
on the change class, providing the complementary supervision signals needed
for accurate delineation of both large building footprints and small isolated
additions. The threshold $\tau = 0.5$, applied at inference time in
Equation~\ref{eq:masking}, and the morphological post-processing operations
complete the pipeline by converting the probabilistic decoder output into the
discrete binary change masks that serve as the basis for quantitative
evaluation in Chapter~\ref{chap:experiments}.
