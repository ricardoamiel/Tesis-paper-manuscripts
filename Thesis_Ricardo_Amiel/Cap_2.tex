\chapter{Theoretical Background}
\label{chap:background}

This chapter establishes the conceptual and mathematical foundations required
to understand the proposed RALA-ChangeFormer architecture. Section~\ref{sec:sat-imagery}
introduces the principles of satellite remote sensing, the orbital and resolution
characteristics relevant to very high-resolution (VHR) urban monitoring, and the
formal problem statement for bi-temporal building change detection.
Section~\ref{sec:siamese-conv} reviews the family of Siamese convolutional
architectures that defined the pre-Transformer era of change detection research,
motivating the transition to attention-based models.
Section~\ref{sec:transformers-attn} derives the full mathematical structure of
multi-head self-attention, including its quadratic time and memory complexity, and
traces its adoption within hierarchical Vision Transformer architectures for Earth
observation. Section~\ref{sec:rala-theory} develops the theoretical basis of
Rank-Augmented Linear Attention~\cite{Fan2025RALA}, beginning with the standard
linear attention formulation, formally characterizing the rank collapse failure mode,
and establishing the rank-augmentation and output-modulation mechanisms that RALA
employs to correct it. Section~\ref{sec:metrics} defines the quantitative evaluation
metrics used throughout the experimental chapters. Section~\ref{sec:bg-final}
synthesizes these foundations and maps them onto the RALA-ChangeFormer design.

\section{Foundations of Remote Sensing and Building Change Detection}
\label{sec:sat-imagery}
% =============================================================================

\subsection{Satellite Imaging Principles}

Satellite remote sensing acquires information about the Earth's surface by
recording electromagnetic radiation---reflected, emitted, or backscattered---
across a range of spectral wavelengths.\footnote{Raya-Armenta, J.~M. et al.\
(2021). A short review of radiation-induced degradation of {III--V}
photovoltaic cells for space applications. \textit{Solar Energy Materials and
Solar Cells}, 233, 111379.
doi:~\url{https://doi.org/10.1016/j.solmat.2021.111379}}
Depending on the sensor design, recorded bands may span the visible spectrum,
near-infrared, thermal infrared, or microwave domains, enabling applications
ranging from crop-health assessment to post-disaster damage mapping.
The operational orbit of a satellite determines its altitude, coverage footprint,
and revisit frequency, and consequently defines the trade-offs available to a
remote sensing mission.

\noindent\textbf{Geostationary Orbit (GEO).}
Geostationary satellites are positioned at approximately 36\,000~km above the
equator, maintaining a fixed position relative to the Earth's surface. Their
constant viewing angle makes them ideal for meteorological monitoring and
telecommunications, but their great altitude limits achievable spatial resolution
to the kilometer scale, precluding building-level analysis.

\noindent\textbf{Low Earth Orbit (LEO).}
Low Earth Orbit satellites operate between approximately 400 and 2\,000~km
altitude and complete several full passes over any given location per day.
Their proximity to Earth enables very high spatial resolution. The Peruvian
national satellite PeruSAT-1, launched in 2016, belongs to this category and
is capable of acquiring panchromatic imagery at 0.70~meters per pixel and
multispectral imagery at 2.80~meters per pixel,\footnote{PeruSAT-1 technical
specifications: panchromatic resolution $\sim$0.70\,m, multispectral resolution
$\sim$2.8\,m. See Airbus PeruSAT-1 datasheet; EO Portal; N2YO.}
a spatial resolution sufficient to delineate individual building footprints,
rooftop configurations, and single-storey additions across large urban extents
in a single orbital pass.

\begin{figure}[h!]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.70\textwidth]{graficos/GEOvsLEO.png}
    \caption{Geostationary Orbit (GEO) versus Low Earth Orbit (LEO) satellites.
    LEO missions such as PeruSAT-1 achieve the high spatial resolution necessary
    for building-level urban change detection.}
    \label{fig:geovsleo}
\end{figure}

The operational utility of a satellite imaging system is characterized along
four resolution dimensions,\footnote{Introduction to Remote Sensing.
\url{https://eol.pages.cms.hu-berlin.de/geo_rs/S02_image-prop1.html}}
each independently constraining the kind of analysis that can be performed:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Spatial resolution:} the ground sampling distance, i.e., the
          side length of the ground area corresponding to one image pixel. VHR
          systems achieve spatial resolutions below 1\,m, enabling structural
          discrimination at the building level.
    \item \textbf{Spectral resolution:} the number and spectral width of the
          recorded bands, governing the capacity to distinguish spectrally similar
          materials (e.g., concrete versus asphalt roofing).
    \item \textbf{Radiometric resolution:} the bit depth of the sensor,
          determining the number of distinct intensity levels that can be recorded
          and hence the dynamic range available for analysis.
    \item \textbf{Temporal resolution:} the revisit period over the same
          geographic area, which governs how frequently bi-temporal image pairs
          can be formed to detect change.
\end{itemize}

    \centering

    \caption{The four resolution dimensions that characterize the operational
    quality of a satellite imaging mission. Spatial resolution is the primary
    determinant of whether individual buildings can be resolved in the imagery.}
    \label{fig:resolutions}
\end{figure}

\subsection{The Bi-Temporal Building Change Detection Problem}
\label{sec:bcd-prelim}

As surveyed by \cite{Hussain2013Review} and \cite{Shafique2022Review},
building change detection (BCD) is a well-established research problem in
remote sensing, with a methodological lineage that extends from early
pixel-differencing and image-ratio approaches to the deep learning
formulations that now define state-of-the-art practice. The task is
formally defined as follows.

Let $I_{t_1}, I_{t_2} \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times C}$ denote a pair of
co-registered satellite images of the same geographic area, acquired at temporal
epochs $t_1 < t_2$, where $H$, $W$, and $C$ denote the spatial height, width,
and number of spectral channels, respectively. The goal of binary BCD is to
produce a change mask
\begin{equation}
Y \in \{0, 1\}^{H \times W},
\label{eq:change_mask}
\end{equation}
where $Y_{ij} = 1$ indicates that a semantically meaningful structural change
has occurred at pixel $(i, j)$ between $t_1$ and $t_2$, and $Y_{ij} = 0$
indicates absence of change. In the context of urban monitoring, change labels
correspond to the appearance, disappearance, or substantial structural
modification of buildings. This formulation reduces BCD to a dense binary
semantic segmentation problem: the model $f_\theta$ must predict $\hat{Y}$
from the concatenated or paired inputs $(I_{t_1}, I_{t_2})$, with parameters
$\theta$ learned from an annotated dataset
$\mathcal{D} = \{(I_{t_1}^{(n)}, I_{t_2}^{(n)}, Y^{(n)})\}_{n=1}^{M}$.

Several intrinsic properties make BCD considerably more difficult than
standard single-image segmentation. The most pervasive is severe class
imbalance: in typical urban VHR datasets, changed pixels constitute only 1--10
percent of the total image area, so the marginal prior probability
$p(Y_{ij} = 1)$ is small and naive models tend toward predicting no-change.
A second challenge is geometric misregistration: even with state-of-the-art
image alignment, residual sub-pixel to multi-pixel spatial offsets between
acquisitions can generate spurious boundary responses that are visually
indistinguishable from genuine structural modifications
\cite{Bandara2022ChangeFormer}. Radiometric inconsistency---caused by
differences in sun angle, atmospheric conditions, sensor gain settings, and
seasonal vegetation state between acquisitions---further complicates the
direct comparison of image pairs. Finally, the scarcity of large-scale,
precisely annotated VHR datasets for cities in the Global South, including
Lima, limits the training and evaluation of models for local operational
deployment.

\begin{figure}[h!]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.65\textwidth]{graficos/LevirCD2.png}
    \caption{Bi-temporal sample from the LEVIR-CD dataset
    \cite{Zheng2020LEVIR}. Left and center panels show the pre-event and
    post-event images; the right panel shows the ground-truth binary change
    mask, where white pixels indicate newly constructed buildings.}
    \label{fig:levircd2}
\end{figure}

The principal public benchmarks used in this thesis span a range of acquisition
geometries, spatial resolutions, and annotation protocols. LEVIR-CD
\cite{Zheng2020LEVIR} contains 637 bi-temporal VHR Google Earth pairs
(1024$\times$1024 pixels) of rapidly developing urban areas. DSIFN-CD
\cite{Zhang2020FusionNetDSIFN} provides multi-class change scenes at
512$\times$512 pixels under heterogeneous imaging conditions. The extended
experimental suite adds WHU-CD \cite{Ji2019WHU}, an aerial centimetre-resolution
dataset with precise building annotations; LEVIR-CD+ \cite{LevirCDPlus},
an augmented version of LEVIR-CD covering wider urban morphologies;
SYSU-CD \cite{ShiSYSU2022}, a 20\,000-pair dataset acquired over nine years
encompassing multi-class surface changes; and S2Looking \cite{ShenS2Looking2021},
a side-looking satellite dataset whose oblique acquisition geometry introduces
perspective distortions that challenge models pretrained on nadir-view imagery.
Together, these six benchmarks provide the evaluation substrate for the
cross-domain experiments in Chapter~\ref{chap:experiments}.

\section{Siamese Convolutional Architectures for Change Detection}
\label{sec:siamese-conv}
% =============================================================================

Prior to the adoption of attention mechanisms, the dominant paradigm for deep
BCD was the \emph{Siamese convolutional network}: a pair of identical,
weight-sharing encoder branches that independently extract features---as
pioneered by \cite{Daudt2018SiamICIP} and extended by \cite{Yang2021DeepSiamese}---
from $I_{t_1}$ and $I_{t_2}$, followed by a change-detection head that compares
the resulting representations. Understanding this family of architectures is
necessary to appreciate the representational limitations that Transformer-based
models were designed to overcome.

\subsection{Fully Convolutional Siamese Networks}

\cite{Daudt2018SiamICIP} introduced three fully convolutional Siamese variants
that established the structural blueprint for subsequent convolutional BCD
research. Let $f_\theta: \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times C} \to
\mathbb{R}^{H' \times W' \times D}$ denote a shared convolutional encoder
with learnable parameters $\theta$. Given the bi-temporal pair, the encoder
produces feature maps
\begin{equation}
F_1 = f_\theta(I_{t_1}), \qquad F_2 = f_\theta(I_{t_2}),
\qquad F_1, F_2 \in \mathbb{R}^{H' \times W' \times D}.
\label{eq:siamese_features}
\end{equation}

The \emph{early-fusion} variant (FC-EF) concatenates the two input images
channel-wise before the encoder, processing the stacked tensor
$[I_{t_1} \| I_{t_2}] \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times 2C}$ through a
single encoder branch. The \emph{late-differencing} variant (FC-Siam-diff)
computes element-wise feature differences
\begin{equation}
\Delta F = F_1 - F_2 \in \mathbb{R}^{H' \times W' \times D},
\label{eq:feature_diff}
\end{equation}
which the decoder then decodes into the binary change mask. The
\emph{concatenation} variant (FC-Siam-conc) concatenates $F_1$ and $F_2$
channel-wise and passes the result to the decoder. All three variants share
the same encoder, and the key insight is that weight-sharing enforces feature
space alignment between temporal branches, making the subsequent comparison
more semantically meaningful than operating on raw pixel differences.

Despite their elegance, fully convolutional Siamese architectures have a
fundamental representational limitation: convolutional operations are
inherently local, with each output neuron attending to a fixed neighborhood
determined by the receptive field. In BCD, where distinguishing a genuine
structural change from a seasonal spectral shift often requires integrating
contextual information from spatially distant regions of the image, this
locality constraint is a severe bottleneck.

\subsection{SNUNet-CD: Densely Connected Siamese Architecture}

SNUNet-CD \cite{Fang2021SNUNetCD} addressed the information loss between
encoder and decoder by introducing a densely connected Siamese architecture
inspired by NestedUNet. Given a Siamese encoder producing multi-scale feature
maps $\{F_1^{(l)}, F_2^{(l)}\}_{l=1}^{L}$ at $L$ resolution levels, SNUNet-CD
constructs an ensemble of dense skip connections between all pairs of encoder
and decoder nodes at the same resolution level, forming a nested feature
aggregation network. At each node $(i, j)$ in the encoder-decoder grid,
the representation is computed as
\begin{equation}
x^{i,j} = \mathcal{H}^{i,j}\!\left(
    x^{i,j-1},\;
    \bigl\{ x^{i+1, k} \bigr\}_{k=0}^{j-1},\;
    \Delta x^{i,j}
\right),
\label{eq:snunet}
\end{equation}
where $x^{i,j-1}$ denotes the up-sampled decoder feature from the previous
node, $x^{i+1, k}$ are the pooled encoder features from the deeper level,
$\Delta x^{i,j}$ is the difference between the two temporal branches at that
node, and $\mathcal{H}^{i,j}$ is a convolutional block. The dense connectivity
pattern reduces the maximum feature propagation path, alleviating the vanishing
gradient problem and preserving fine-grained spatial details that standard
U-Net skip connections tend to lose.

\subsection{DSIFN-CD: Deep Fusion for Multi-Scale Comparison}

\cite{Zhang2020FusionNetDSIFN} proposed DSIFN-CD (Deeply Supervised Image
Fusion Network), which introduced an explicit deep supervision strategy to
guide multi-scale feature comparison in the DSIFN-CD dataset. The architecture
processes bi-temporal features through a series of fusion modules that compute
channel-wise attention weights from the concatenated representation
$[F_1^{(l)} \| F_2^{(l)}]$ at each scale $l$, modulating the relative
contribution of each channel to the change map. Intermediate change predictions
are generated at multiple decoder levels and supervised independently with
auxiliary losses, enforcing that semantically meaningful change signals are
present in the feature hierarchy from early training iterations.

\begin{figure}[h!]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{plots/DSIFN2.pdf}
    \caption{Multi-temporal image samples and binary ground-truth change masks
    from the DSIFN-CD benchmark \cite{Zhang2020FusionNetDSIFN}. Each row
    displays the pre-event image (left), the co-registered post-event image
    (centre), and the annotated binary change mask (right). The dataset's
    heterogeneous imaging conditions and multi-class land-cover changes make
    it a demanding benchmark for evaluating deep multi-scale supervision
    strategies in building change detection.}
    \label{fig:dsifn_samples}
\end{figure}

The evolution from FC-EF through SNUNet-CD and DSIFN-CD established that
expressive feature comparison mechanisms and multi-scale information
preservation are essential for competitive BCD performance. However, all of
these architectures remained constrained by the local receptive field of
convolutions, unable to model the long-range spatial dependencies that
distinguish genuine structural changes from coincidental spectral similarity.
This limitation motivated the adoption of Transformer-based architectures.

\section{Vision Transformers in Earth Observation}
\label{sec:transformers-attn}
% =============================================================================

\subsection{Multi-Head Self-Attention: Formulation and Complexity}

The Transformer architecture \cite{Vaswani2017Attention} introduced the
\emph{self-attention} mechanism as a universal tool for modeling pairwise
dependencies among elements of a sequence, without any locality constraint.
Given an input token sequence represented as a matrix
$X \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d}$, where $N$ is the number of tokens and $d$
is the embedding dimension, self-attention first computes three linear
projections:
\begin{equation}
Q = X W_Q, \qquad K = X W_K, \qquad V = X W_V,
\label{eq:qkv}
\end{equation}
where $W_Q, W_K, W_V \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$ are learned projection
matrices. The query matrix $Q \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d}$, key matrix
$K \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d}$, and value matrix $V \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d}$
encode, respectively, the information a token seeks, the information it
provides as a retrieval target, and the information it contributes to the
output.

\noindent\textbf{Softmax attention.}
The scaled dot-product attention output is defined as
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Attn}(Q, K, V)
    = \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\frac{Q K^\top}{\sqrt{d}}\right) V,
\label{eq:softmax_attention}
\end{equation}
where the division by $\sqrt{d}$ stabilizes the variance of the dot products
prior to the softmax normalization. The intermediate matrix
$A = \mathrm{softmax}(Q K^\top / \sqrt{d}) \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times N}$
is the \emph{attention map}: each row $A_{i,:}$ encodes a probability
distribution over all $N$ tokens, describing the relative importance of
every token as a context source for token $i$. The final output is a
weighted sum of value vectors, integrating global contextual information
from the entire sequence into each token representation.

\noindent\textbf{Quadratic computational complexity.}
Forming the full attention map $A$ requires computing the $N \times N$
matrix product $Q K^\top$, which has time complexity $\mathcal{O}(N^2 d)$
and demands $\mathcal{O}(N^2)$ memory for storage. In multi-head attention
with $h$ independent heads, the total cost scales as $\mathcal{O}(h N^2 d)$,
though reducing the per-head dimension to $d/h$ keeps the dominant term as
$\mathcal{O}(N^2)$ in memory. The practical consequence for VHR building
change detection is severe: a $256 \times 256$ patch yields $N = 65\,536$
spatial tokens at the finest encoder stage, and a bi-temporal pipeline
concatenating two such sequences before the attention module doubles the
effective sequence length to $N = 131\,072$ tokens---a regime in which the
$\mathcal{O}(N^2)$ memory demand becomes prohibitive under standard GPU
memory budgets of 16--40~GB.

    \caption{Multi-Head Self-Attention (MHSA) mechanism. Each head computes
    independent query, key, and value projections, forming a dense $N\times N$

\subsection{Hierarchical Vision Transformers and ChangeFormer}

\cite{Dosovitskiy2021ViT} introduced the Vision Transformer (ViT), which
partitions an image into a grid of non-overlapping patches, linearly embeds
each patch into a token vector, and applies a standard Transformer encoder.
The pure sequence processing of ViT treats the image as a flat token set,
which is effective for image classification but requires high-resolution inputs
to be significantly downsampled before patching, limiting spatial detail
in dense prediction tasks.

\cite{Liu2021Swin} proposed the Swin Transformer, which reintroduces spatial
inductive biases into the Transformer framework through a hierarchical feature
pyramid and a shifted-window attention strategy. Swin computes self-attention
within local, non-overlapping windows of fixed size $M \times M$, reducing
the per-layer attention complexity from $\mathcal{O}(N^2)$ to
$\mathcal{O}(M^2 N)$, where $M \ll N$. Cross-window interaction is achieved
by cyclically shifting the partition between consecutive layers. The hierarchical
design produces multi-scale feature maps analogous to convolutional pyramids,
enabling dense prediction without the quadratic cost of global attention.

\cite{Bandara2022ChangeFormer} introduced ChangeFormer, the primary baseline
for this thesis. ChangeFormer is a hierarchical Siamese encoder-decoder
architecture that processes bi-temporal VHR images $I_{t_1}$ and $I_{t_2}$
through shared convolutional-Transformer encoder blocks, producing multi-scale
feature maps
\begin{equation}
F_{t_1}^{(l)},\; F_{t_2}^{(l)} \in \mathbb{R}^{H_l \times W_l \times C_l},
\quad l = 1, \ldots, L,
\label{eq:multiscale_features}
\end{equation}
where $H_l$ and $W_l$ decrease and $C_l$ increases with depth level $l$.
At each scale $l$, the bi-temporal feature maps are concatenated channel-wise
and projected into a token sequence via a lightweight patch embedding:
\begin{equation}
X^{(l)} = \mathrm{PatchEmbed}\!\left(F_{t_1}^{(l)} \,\Vert\, F_{t_2}^{(l)}\right)
    \in \mathbb{R}^{N_l \times C_l},
\quad N_l = H_l W_l.
\label{eq:patchembedding}
\end{equation}
Each token sequence $X^{(l)}$ is then processed by a Transformer block
containing MHSA followed by a feed-forward network (FFN), which refines the
joint bi-temporal representation before the decoder fuses and upsamples the
multi-scale outputs into the binary change mask. Because ChangeFormer applies
MHSA globally at each scale without the windowed restriction of Swin, the
quadratic cost $\mathcal{O}(N_l^2)$ is paid in full at every encoder level,
making the decoder the dominant computational bottleneck for high-resolution
inputs.

\section{Rank-Augmented Linear Attention Theory}
\label{sec:rala-theory}
% =============================================================================

\subsection{Linear Attention and the Key-Value Buffer}

A principled approach to reducing the quadratic cost of softmax attention is to
replace the softmax kernel with a positive-definite feature map
$\kappa: \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}^{d'}$, such that
$\kappa(q)^\top \kappa(k) \approx \exp(q^\top k / \sqrt{d})$ for all
$q, k \in \mathbb{R}^d$. This substitution, introduced by
\cite{Katharopoulos2020Linear}, yields the \emph{linear attention} formulation:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Attn}_{\mathrm{lin}}(Q, K, V)
    = \kappa(Q)\,\bigl(\kappa(K)^\top V\bigr),
\label{eq:linear_attention}
\end{equation}
where $\kappa(Q) \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d}$ and
$\kappa(K) \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d}$ are the row-wise applications of
$\kappa$ to $Q$ and $K$. The critical algebraic observation is that matrix
multiplication is associative: rather than computing the $N \times N$
intermediate product $\kappa(Q)\kappa(K)^\top$ at cost $\mathcal{O}(N^2 d)$,
one can first compute
\begin{equation}
B = \kappa(K)^\top V \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d},
\label{eq:kv_buffer}
\end{equation}
at cost $\mathcal{O}(N d^2)$, and then compute each output token as
$\kappa(Q_i) B$ at cost $\mathcal{O}(N d^2)$, reducing the total attention
computation from $\mathcal{O}(N^2 d)$ to $\mathcal{O}(N d^2)$. Since $d$ is
a fixed architectural hyperparameter (e.g., $d = 64$ per head) while
$N$ scales with image resolution, this reduction is asymptotically from
$\mathcal{O}(N^2)$ to $\mathcal{O}(N)$ in the dominant term. The matrix $B$
in Equation~\ref{eq:kv_buffer} serves as a global \emph{key-value buffer}
that aggregates information from the entire token sequence into a compact
$d \times d$ summary.

Related efficient attention approaches extend this idea through different
approximation strategies: Performers \cite{Choromanski2021Performer} use random
feature maps to approximate the softmax kernel, Linformer
\cite{Wang2020Linformer} projects the key and value matrices into a
lower-dimensional space, Nystr\"{o}mformer \cite{Xiong2021Nystromformer}
approximates the attention matrix via Nystr\"{o}m decomposition, and Swin
Transformer \cite{Liu2021Swin} restricts self-attention to local windows.
Sparsifiner \cite{Wei2023Sparsifiner} learns instance-dependent sparse
attention patterns. Despite their theoretical appeal, these methods share a
common failure mode under dense prediction settings.

\subsection{Rank Collapse in the Key-Value Buffer}

The fundamental pathology of standard linear attention in dense prediction is
\emph{rank collapse} of the key-value buffer $B$. In practice, the key vectors
$K_j \in \mathbb{R}^d$ for $j = 1, \ldots, N$ tend to cluster in a
low-dimensional subspace due to the shared encoder weights and the similar
spatial statistics of neighboring tokens in an image. When many key vectors
are nearly collinear, the outer products $\kappa(K_j)^\top V_j$ in the
accumulation
\begin{equation}
B = \sum_{j=1}^{N} \kappa(K_j)^\top V_j
\label{eq:standard_kv_buffer}
\end{equation}
span only a small subspace of $\mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$, resulting in a
buffer of effective rank $r \ll d$. A low-rank buffer is structurally
equivalent to a lossy projection: the global context representation $B$
discards the spatial diversity of the token sequence, collapsing it into a
compressed summary that cannot distinguish fine-grained structural differences
between building edges, construction materials, or spatial configurations.
In the context of BCD, where precise boundary delineation at sub-building
spatial granularity is required to accurately segment newly constructed
structures, this rank collapse translates directly into degraded segmentation
accuracy and loss of change boundary precision. This failure mode, which
\cite{Fan2025RALA} formally characterizes across multiple dense prediction
benchmarks, was consistently observed in earlier linear attention variants.

\subsection{RALA: Rank Augmentation via Global Query Weighting}

\cite{Fan2025RALA} addresses rank collapse through a two-component mechanism:
a learned, token-wise reweighting of key-value contributions to the buffer,
and a channel-wise output modulation that restores per-token feature diversity.
Together, these components constitute Rank-Augmented Linear Attention (RALA).

The first component augments the rank of $B$ by replacing the uniform token
aggregation in Equation~\ref{eq:standard_kv_buffer} with a
\emph{token-relevance-weighted} accumulation. RALA introduces a
\emph{global query descriptor}:
\begin{equation}
Q_g = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} Q_i \in \mathbb{R}^{1 \times d},
\label{eq:global_query}
\end{equation}
which encodes the average semantic query intent of the entire token sequence.
Using $Q_g$ as a probe, RALA computes a scalar relevance weight for each key
$K_j$ via a softmax normalization:
\begin{equation}
\alpha_j = N \times
    \frac{\exp\!\left(Q_g \,\kappa(K_j)^\top\right)}
    {\displaystyle\sum_{m=1}^{N} \exp\!\left(Q_g \,\kappa(K_m)^\top\right)},
\label{eq:alpha_softmax}
\end{equation}
where the factor of $N$ preserves the expected magnitude of the buffer. The
weight $\alpha_j \in \mathbb{R}_{>0}$ is large for tokens whose key $K_j$
is semantically aligned with the global query descriptor $Q_g$, and small for
tokens whose keys are weakly relevant. The rank-augmented key-value buffer
is then:
\begin{equation}
B = \sum_{j=1}^{N} \alpha_j \, \kappa(K_j)^\top V_j \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}.
\label{eq:rala_buffer}
\end{equation}
Because information-rich tokens receive disproportionately large weights, the
effective contribution of each token to $B$ is no longer uniform. Key vectors
from semantically distinct spatial locations contribute distinct outer products
$\kappa(K_j)^\top V_j$ with amplified magnitudes, increasing the span of the
accumulated outer products and thereby increasing $\mathrm{rank}(B)$ relative
to the standard uniform accumulation in Equation~\ref{eq:standard_kv_buffer}.

\subsection{Output Feature Modulation and the Rank Inequality}

Even when the rank-augmentation mechanism of Equation~\ref{eq:rala_buffer}
raises $\mathrm{rank}(B)$ to its maximum value of $d$, the output features
obtained from
\begin{equation}
Z_i = \kappa(Q_i)\, B \in \mathbb{R}^{d}
\label{eq:Z_pre_modulation}
\end{equation}
may still occupy a low-rank subspace. This follows from a fundamental matrix
rank inequality. Let $\tilde{Q} = [\kappa(Q_1)^\top, \ldots, \kappa(Q_N)^\top]^\top
\in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d}$. Then the output matrix
$Z = \tilde{Q} B$ satisfies
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{rank}(Z)
    \leq \min\!\bigl(\mathrm{rank}(\tilde{Q}),\; \mathrm{rank}(B)\bigr)
    \leq d.
\label{eq:rank_inequality}
\end{equation}
This inequality establishes that the expressible rank of the output cannot
exceed the rank of the more restrictive factor. In remote sensing imagery,
the query vectors $Q_i = X_i W_Q$ are derived from convolutional or
patch-embedding token features that exhibit strong spatial autocorrelation:
adjacent pixels share similar textures, and entire homogeneous building regions
generate nearly identical query representations. Consequently,
$\mathrm{rank}(\tilde{Q}) \ll d$, and the inequality in
Equation~\ref{eq:rank_inequality} guarantees that $\mathrm{rank}(Z) \ll d$
even when $B$ is full-rank, resulting in a collapsed output that cannot
represent the per-token spatial diversity required for accurate boundary
segmentation.

RALA corrects this structural deficiency through a \emph{token-wise output
modulation} step. Rather than using $Z_i$ directly as the attention output,
RALA applies a token-specific transform $\phi(\cdot)$ to the original input
token $X_i$ and combines it with $Z_i$ via the Hadamard (element-wise) product:
\begin{equation}
Y_i = \phi(X_i) \odot \bigl(\kappa(Q_i)\, B\bigr),
\label{eq:Yi_modulated}
\end{equation}
where $\phi: \mathbb{R}^{d} \to \mathbb{R}^{d}$ is a learned per-token
transformation (implemented as a lightweight gated linear unit applied
channel-wise) and $\odot$ denotes the Hadamard product. The term $\phi(X_i)$
injects token-specific local information into the output, effectively acting
as a channel-wise gate that modulates the global context signal $\kappa(Q_i) B$
with local per-token features. Because $\phi(X_i)$ differs for each token $i$
by construction, the modulated output matrix
$Y = [\phi(X_1) \odot Z_1, \ldots, \phi(X_N) \odot Z_N]^\top$ can achieve
a rank up to $d$ independently of $\mathrm{rank}(\tilde{Q})$, breaking the
constraint imposed by Equation~\ref{eq:rank_inequality}. Critically, the
Hadamard product and $\phi(\cdot)$ operate channel-wise without introducing
any $N \times N$ pairwise computation, so the overall time and memory
complexity of RALA remains $\mathcal{O}(N d^2)$---linear in $N$ and independent
of the quadratic bottleneck of standard softmax attention.

\section{Evaluation Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}
% =============================================================================

Building change detection is evaluated as a binary dense prediction task in
which the positive class corresponds to changed pixels and the negative class
to unchanged pixels. The standard performance metrics are derived from the
entries of the pixel-level confusion matrix: true positives ($\mathrm{TP}$),
false positives ($\mathrm{FP}$), true negatives ($\mathrm{TN}$), and false
negatives ($\mathrm{FN}$).

\noindent\textbf{Precision} measures the fraction of predicted-positive pixels
that are genuinely changed:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Precision}
    = \frac{\mathrm{TP}}{\mathrm{TP} + \mathrm{FP}}.
\label{eq:precision}
\end{equation}
A high precision indicates that the model rarely labels unchanged pixels as
changed, at the potential cost of missing some genuine changes.

\noindent\textbf{Recall} (also called sensitivity or true positive rate)
measures the fraction of genuinely changed pixels that are correctly detected:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Recall}
    = \frac{\mathrm{TP}}{\mathrm{TP} + \mathrm{FN}}.
\label{eq:recall}
\end{equation}
A high recall indicates that the model detects most real changes, potentially
at the cost of an elevated false alarm rate.

\noindent\textbf{F1-Score} is the harmonic mean of precision and recall,
providing a single scalar summary that balances both:
\begin{equation}
F_1
    = \frac{2 \cdot \mathrm{Precision} \cdot \mathrm{Recall}}
           {\mathrm{Precision} + \mathrm{Recall}}
    = \frac{2\,\mathrm{TP}}{2\,\mathrm{TP} + \mathrm{FP} + \mathrm{FN}}.
\label{eq:f1}
\end{equation}
The F1-score is the primary accuracy metric in this thesis because it directly
addresses the class imbalance between changed and unchanged pixels: a model
that trivially predicts no-change everywhere achieves $F_1 = 0$, regardless
of the high accuracy that such a strategy would produce on a pixel-accuracy
metric.

\noindent\textbf{Intersection over Union (IoU)} is defined as the ratio of
the area of intersection to the area of union between the predicted change
mask $\hat{Y}$ and the ground-truth mask $Y$:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{IoU}
    = \frac{\mathrm{TP}}{\mathrm{TP} + \mathrm{FP} + \mathrm{FN}}.
\label{eq:iou}
\end{equation}
IoU is geometrically interpretable as the overlap coefficient of two binary
sets and is widely used in semantic segmentation tasks. It is a stricter metric
than $F_1$, penalizing both missed detections and false alarms more severely
for the same absolute counts of $\mathrm{FP}$ and $\mathrm{FN}$.

The relationship between these metrics is illustrated in
Figure~\ref{fig:metrics}: the precision-recall trade-off defines the
operational performance envelope of a change detection model, with the $F_1$
score marking the point of balanced performance and the IoU providing a
complementary geometric measure of localization accuracy.

\section{Final Considerations}
\label{sec:bg-final}
% =============================================================================

The theoretical material presented in this chapter establishes the complete
formal substrate required to understand the proposed RALA-ChangeFormer
architecture. The satellite imaging framework of Section~\ref{sec:sat-imagery}
motivates the specific technical requirements of the approach: VHR spatial
resolution is necessary to resolve building-level structural changes, and
bi-temporal acquisitions over Lima Metropolitan Area via PeruSAT-1 define the
target operational domain. The BCD problem formulation in
Section~\ref{sec:bcd-prelim} establishes that accurate, spatially precise
change masks are required, making dense binary segmentation the appropriate
task formulation and $F_1$ and IoU the appropriate evaluation metrics.

The convolutional Siamese architectures reviewed in
Section~\ref{sec:siamese-conv} illustrate the representational limitation that
motivates the Transformer paradigm: the local receptive field of convolutions
prevents these models from integrating the broad spatial context necessary to
disambiguate genuine structural changes from radiometric artifacts. The
hierarchical Vision Transformer analysis of Section~\ref{sec:transformers-attn}
demonstrates that attention-based architectures resolve this locality limitation
but introduce a new practical constraint: the quadratic $\mathcal{O}(N^2)$
memory cost of MHSA renders full-resolution VHR processing infeasible under
standard GPU budgets, specifically limiting ChangeFormer to small batch sizes
and preventing systematic evaluation at native 1024$\times$1024 resolution.

The RALA theory of Section~\ref{sec:rala-theory} establishes that this
quadratic bottleneck can be eliminated through rank-augmented linear attention
without accuracy degradation, provided that both the KV buffer and the output
features are actively protected against rank collapse through the global query
reweighting mechanism and the Hadamard modulation step. The formal rank
inequality of Equation~\ref{eq:rank_inequality} makes precise why both
components are necessary: augmenting the rank of $B$ alone is insufficient
when $\mathrm{rank}(\kappa(Q)) < d$, which is the typical condition for
spatially correlated remote sensing tokens. Chapter~\ref{chap:method}
describes how this integrated mechanism is concretely incorporated into
the ChangeFormer encoder, and Chapter~\ref{chap:experiments} provides
the empirical verification of the resulting accuracy-efficiency trade-offs
across the six-dataset benchmark suite.
