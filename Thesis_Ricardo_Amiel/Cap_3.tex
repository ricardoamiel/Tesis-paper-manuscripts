\chapter{Related Work}
\label{chap:related_work}

The development of RALA-ChangeFormer sits at the intersection of three
sustained research trajectories: the long evolution of automated building
change detection from classical image analysis to deep neural architectures,
the more recent paradigm shift toward Transformer-based bi-temporal reasoning
in Earth observation, and the parallel effort to reduce the computational cost
of self-attention without sacrificing the representational fidelity that dense
prediction tasks demand. The present chapter traces these trajectories in
sequence, drawing out the specific limitations of each prior generation of
methods that motivate the next, and culminating in a precise characterization
of the evaluation gap---across acquisition geometries, spatial resolutions,
and scene complexities---that the experimental program of this thesis is
designed to close.

\section{Traditional and Convolutional Change Detection Paradigms}
\label{sec:supervised-bcd}
% =============================================================================

The intellectual origins of automated change detection predate the deep
learning era by several decades. Classical approaches operated directly on
pixel intensity values, As surveyed by \cite{Hussain2013Review}, classical approaches produced change
indicators through image differencing, image rationing, or post-classification
comparison of independently labelled single-date images. Image differencing---subtracting
the pixel values of a reference image from those of a query image and
thresholding the residual---is computationally trivial but sensitive to any
radiometric inconsistency between acquisitions, including differences in solar
zenith angle, atmospheric aerosol loading, and sensor calibration drift. Since
none of these sources of variation are semantically meaningful, classical
difference maps contain a large proportion of false alarms that cannot be
suppressed without laborious manual thresholding. Post-classification
comparison partially mitigates this problem by applying independent land-cover
classifiers to each date before comparing the resulting label maps; however,
classification errors in either single-date map propagate into the change
product, and the approach requires two separately trained classifiers whose
label ontologies must be exactly harmonized.

The introduction of convolutional neural networks for dense prediction tasks
in remote sensing opened a substantially more powerful pathway by enabling
end-to-end learning of change-discriminative features directly from raw image
pairs \cite{Shafique2022Review}. The foundational architecture in this context
is the fully convolutional Siamese network, in which two branches with shared
weights process the two temporal acquisitions independently, and a comparison
module---applied to the resulting feature maps---produces the dense change
prediction. \cite{Daudt2018SiamICIP} established the canonical design space
for this family by proposing three variants: FC-EF, which concatenates the
input images channel-wise and feeds the joint tensor into a single encoder;
FC-Siam-Diff, which encodes the two images through a shared branch and
computes the element-wise difference of corresponding feature maps; and
FC-Siam-Conc, which concatenates those feature maps channel-wise before the
decoder. The weight-sharing constraint in FC-Siam-Diff and FC-Siam-Conc is
crucial: because both branches apply identical linear transformations to their
respective inputs, the resulting feature representations are guaranteed to
occupy the same metric space, making the difference or concatenation
semantically coherent as a change signal. FC-EF, by contrast, presents the
model with a joint input and relies on the encoder to learn the temporal
comparison implicitly, which sacrifices this geometric guarantee but enables
richer early-fusion interactions between the two temporal fields.

Beyond the architecture of the backbone, a pervasive challenge in convolutional
BCD is the management of spatial detail across the encoder hierarchy. Standard
encoder-decoder networks with pooling operations progressively compress spatial
resolution, and while this compression enables the model to build high-level
semantic representations, it tends to discard the fine edge information that
is essential for precise building boundary delineation. SNUNet-CD
\cite{Fang2021SNUNetCD} addressed this problem by introducing a dense skip
connection strategy adapted from the NestedUNet architecture. Rather than
connecting each encoder level to only the spatially corresponding decoder
level, SNUNet-CD employs a complete connection graph in which every decoder
node receives features from all encoder nodes at the same or higher spatial
resolution, maintaining a continuous flow of fine-grained spatial detail
throughout the decoding process. This architectural choice proved particularly
effective for detecting small, isolated building additions that would otherwise
be lost in the compressed encoder representation.

A complementary line of work focused on the design of the comparison module
itself. Deep supervision was identified as a powerful training signal for
enforcing that semantically meaningful change information is present at every
level of the feature hierarchy, not only at the decoder output. DSIFN-CD
\cite{Zhang2020FusionNetDSIFN} applied this strategy within a fusion network
designed for the heterogeneous imaging conditions of the DSIFN-CD benchmark,
where multi-class land-cover changes at varying spatial scales require the
model to simultaneously integrate coarse semantic context and fine texture
detail. Channel attention mechanisms applied to the concatenated bi-temporal
feature maps at each scale selectively amplify the channels most informative
for change discrimination while suppressing channels dominated by consistent
background structure. Subsequent architectures, including
FCCDN \cite{PChen2022FCCDN} and HFA-Net \cite{Zheng2022HFANet}, refined this
approach by incorporating high-frequency spectral components into the attention
weighting mechanism, reasoning that genuine structural changes---newly
constructed concrete surfaces, exposed roofing materials, bare excavation
ground---tend to produce sharper high-frequency boundaries than the temporally
consistent gradients of vegetation and paved surfaces.

Despite this progression, the convolutional paradigm carries a fundamental
representational limitation that no amount of architectural refinement can
fully overcome: the receptive field of any convolutional layer is bounded by
the product of its kernel size and the number of preceding downsampling
operations. In VHR imagery, where a single patch of 256$\times$256 pixels can
contain dozens of individual buildings embedded in a complex urban fabric of
roads, vegetation, and shadow, the ability to reason about the spatial
relationship between a newly constructed building and distant contextual
structures---a neighboring construction site, a road intersection, a
previously demolished building---requires integrating information across
spatial extents that exceed the effective receptive field of any practically
feasible convolutional architecture. This global context dependency is not a
secondary consideration: urban growth in cities like Lima is characterized by
construction clusters, where the appearance of new buildings in one location
predicts heightened probability of change in geometrically distant but
functionally related areas. Convolutional models, constrained to local
interactions, cannot represent these non-local spatial dependencies and
therefore produce change maps that are locally accurate but globally
incoherent.

\section{The Attention Era in Multi-Temporal Remote Sensing}
\label{sec:enablers-transformers}
% =============================================================================

The introduction of self-attention---formalized by \cite{Vaswani2017Attention}
as the primary computation unit in sequence modelling---and its subsequent
adaptation to computer vision \cite{Dosovitskiy2021ViT} provided remote
sensing researchers
with a principled mechanism for modeling non-local spatial dependencies that
convolutional architectures cannot capture. In the Transformer framework,
every token in the input sequence explicitly attends to every other token via
pairwise dot-product interactions, constructing a dense attention map that
encodes the full relational structure of the spatial context. This global
receptive field is available at every layer of the encoder, without requiring
the progressive expansion of convolutional stacks, and it does not depend on
any spatial locality assumption. For building change detection, where genuine
construction events must be discriminated from radiometric artifacts
distributed across the entire image, this representational universality is a
qualitative rather than merely quantitative advance.

The first significant application of Transformer-based reasoning to BCD was
the Binary change detection with Image Transformers (BIT) architecture
\cite{Chen2022BIT}. BIT processes the bi-temporal feature maps produced by a
convolutional backbone through a compact semantic token set---a small number
of prototype vectors that summarize the high-level content of each
acquisition---and applies cross-temporal attention between these tokens to
model the relational structure of the temporal change. The cross-temporal
interaction mechanism of BIT is conceptually powerful: by attending from query
tokens derived from $I_{t_2}$ to key-value pairs derived from $I_{t_1}$,
the architecture explicitly models the asymmetric relationship between a
reference state and an updated state, rather than treating both acquisitions
symmetrically. This directional design proved effective on the LEVIR-CD and
WHU-CD benchmarks \cite{Ji2019WHU}, and BIT's lightweight token compression
reduced the sequence length processed by the Transformer to a manageable
constant, sidestepping the quadratic scaling of full self-attention at the
cost of limiting the resolution of the modeled spatial relationships to the
granularity of the learned prototypes.

ChangeFormer \cite{Bandara2022ChangeFormer} constituted a more principled and
architecturally complete adoption of hierarchical Vision Transformers for BCD.
Rather than limiting Transformer processing to a compressed token
representation, ChangeFormer applied full multi-head self-attention at each
level of a hierarchical Siamese encoder whose design follows the SegFormer-B2
backbone. The bi-temporal inputs are processed through shared patch embedding
modules at four resolution levels, and the resulting token sequences of length
$N_l = H_l \times W_l$ at level $l$ are jointly processed by MHSA blocks
that model temporal and spatial dependencies simultaneously. At each scale,
the joint representation is decoded by a cascaded Transformer block before
upsampling and feature fusion produce the final binary change mask.
ChangeFormer demonstrated state-of-the-art accuracy on the LEVIR-CD benchmark
and established the hierarchical Siamese Transformer as the reference design
for subsequent work in the field.

The underlying mechanism that drives the representational advantage of
ChangeFormer and BIT over their convolutional predecessors can be understood
through the properties of the attention map. For each query token at position
$i$, the row $A_{i,:}$ of the attention matrix encodes a learned distribution
over the entire input sequence, assigning high weight to tokens that are
semantically or geometrically most informative for the query. In the context
of BCD, this means that a query token located at a changing building boundary
can attend to tokens from distant regions of the same or the other temporal
image that provide disambiguating context---for example, the consistent
spectral signature of a nearby unchanged structure that rules out illumination
as the cause of the observed spectral difference. This long-range
disambiguation is unavailable to convolutional models and constitutes the
primary driver of the performance gap between the two paradigms.

HFA-Net \cite{Zheng2022HFANet} demonstrated that the benefit of
attention-based context modeling extends to spectral as well as spatial
reasoning. By incorporating high-frequency attention modules that selectively
weight high-frequency spectral components of the feature representation,
HFA-Net improved boundary delineation on complex urban scenes where
low-frequency dominant attention fails to resolve fine-grained rooftop edge
signals. Swin-CD \cite{Teng2023SwinCD} applied the Swin Transformer backbone
\cite{Liu2021Swin} to the bi-temporal BCD problem, exploiting the
shifted-window partition mechanism to reduce the computational cost of
attention while preserving local spatial context at each resolution level.
These architectural innovations confirmed the generality of the
attention-based BCD paradigm and established a clear empirical hierarchy:
for urban VHR imagery, hierarchical Transformer architectures consistently
outperform convolutional Siamese networks given comparable training data and
supervision.

However, the superior representational capacity of full multi-head
self-attention comes with a computational cost that severely limits its
applicability in practice. Standard MHSA forms the dense $N \times N$
attention matrix $A = \mathrm{softmax}(QK^\top/\sqrt{d})$, whose construction
requires $\mathcal{O}(N^2)$ time and memory. In bi-temporal BCD, where both
acquisitions contribute tokens to the sequence processed by each attention
block, the effective sequence length is doubled relative to single-image
segmentation, further amplifying the quadratic cost. For VHR imagery at
sub-meter resolution, the number of spatial tokens $N_l$ at the finest
encoder stage can exceed tens of thousands, pushing the $N^2$ attention matrix
beyond the memory capacity of standard research GPUs. This constraint is
particularly acute for institutions engaged in Earth observation for development
applications in Latin America and the Global South, where access to large-scale
GPU clusters cannot be assumed.

\section{Efficient Attention and Low-Rank Limits in Dense Vision}
\label{sec:ssl-semi-unsup}
% =============================================================================

The practical limitations of standard MHSA motivated an extensive research
programme on efficient attention approximations that reduce computational
complexity from quadratic to near-linear in the sequence length $N$. These
methods can be broadly categorized into three families: kernel-based linear
approximations, low-rank matrix factorizations, and sparse or window-restricted
attention variants.


The kernel-based approach reformulates the softmax attention kernel $\exp(q^\top k / \sqrt{d})$. Following the structural linear attention formulation originally, who is introduced by \cite{Katharopoulos2020Linear}, this kernel is mapped as the inner product of two positive feature map vectors $\kappa(q)^\top \kappa(k)$, where $\kappa: \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}^{d'}$ represents a fixed or learned transformation. This substitution enables the key-value product
$\kappa(K)^\top V$ to be precomputed as a global $d' \times d$ buffer,
reducing the per-query output computation to a single matrix-vector product
and bringing total attention complexity down to $\mathcal{O}(N {d'}^2)$,
linear in $N$. The Performer \cite{Choromanski2021Performer} operationalized
this approach using random Fourier features as the kernel approximation,
providing theoretical guarantees on the accuracy of the softmax approximation
in expectation. Despite the theoretical appeal of these methods, practical
deployments revealed a systematic accuracy degradation on dense prediction
tasks relative to the softmax baseline, a degradation that intensifies as the
spatial resolution and feature diversity of the input increase.

The low-rank family pursues a different strategy: rather than replacing the
attention kernel, these methods project the key or value matrices into a
compressed subspace of dimension $r \ll N$ before computing the attention
map. Linformer \cite{Wang2020Linformer} projects $K$ and $V$ into
$r$-dimensional spaces using learned linear projections, reducing the
attention matrix to an $N \times r$ object whose computation costs
$\mathcal{O}(Nr)$. Nystr\"{o}mformer \cite{Xiong2021Nystromformer} approximates
the full attention matrix via the Nystr\"{o}m decomposition, selecting $r$
landmark tokens from the input sequence and expressing the full $N \times N$
attention in terms of three smaller matrices of size $N \times r$ and
$r \times r$. These approaches achieve genuine reductions in computational
cost but depend on the assumption that the attention matrix is
well-approximated by a low-rank factorization---an assumption that is
reasonable for classification tasks, where the pooled global representation
is the target, but problematic for dense prediction tasks, where the model
must produce an independent output for each of the $N$ tokens.

The sparse and windowed attention family restricts attention interactions to
spatially local windows of fixed size $M \times M$---a design pioneered by
the Swin Transformer \cite{Liu2021Swin}---reducing per-layer complexity
to $\mathcal{O}(M^2 N)$ with $M \ll N$. Cross-window
interaction is achieved through cyclic window shifts in alternating layers.
Sparsifiner \cite{Wei2023Sparsifiner} extends this idea to learned,
instance-dependent sparse patterns, where the model predicts which token
pairs to attend to based on a lightweight routing network. While windowed and
sparse attention preserve local spatial precision within each window, they
reintroduce a locality constraint that limits the effective receptive field
of each layer---effectively trading the global context capability of full
attention for computational tractability. In BCD, this trade-off is
particularly costly because disambiguation of change events often requires
integrating information from spatially distant image regions.

The unifying theoretical pathology underlying all kernel-based and low-rank
efficient attention methods in dense vision is \emph{rank collapse} of the
key-value aggregation buffer. When the token-wise contributions to the global
key-value summary are accumulated uniformly---without differentiating between
informationally rich and redundant tokens---the resulting buffer occupies a
low-dimensional subspace of the feature space, discarding the spatial diversity
that is essential for per-token output discrimination. A formal characterization
of this phenomenon, provided in \cite{Fan2025RALA}, demonstrates through a
matrix rank inequality that the output feature matrix is bounded above in rank
by the minimum of the query feature matrix rank and the buffer rank. In remote
sensing imagery, where spatial autocorrelation is high within homogeneous
surface covers such as concrete roofing and dense vegetation, the query feature
matrix $\kappa(Q)$ is structurally low-rank, guaranteeing collapsed outputs
even when the buffer itself achieves full rank. This result explains the
consistent empirical finding that linear attention variants degrade in accuracy
relative to softmax attention in proportion to the spatial resolution and scene
heterogeneity of the benchmark---a pattern that is particularly pronounced in
VHR BCD, where precise boundary localization depends on the diversity of
per-token output representations.

Rank-Augmented Linear Attention (RALA), as formalized by \cite{Fan2025RALA},
addresses this pathology through two complementary mechanisms. The global
query descriptor, computed as the mean of all query vectors, is used to derive
token-wise relevance weights that differentially amplify the contribution of
informationally distinct tokens to the buffer accumulation. This breaks the
uniform aggregation assumption of standard linear attention and increases the
effective rank of the buffer. A token-wise output modulation---implemented as
a Hadamard product between the attention output and a learned per-token gating
signal---then injects local per-token information into the final representation,
directly increasing the expressive rank of the output matrix beyond the bound
imposed by the query feature rank. Together, these two mechanisms allow RALA
to achieve near-linear computational complexity in $N$ while maintaining the
representational fidelity required for accurate dense prediction. The
theoretical derivation of these mechanisms is presented in full in
Section~\ref{sec:rala-theory}, and their integration into the ChangeFormer
encoder is described in Chapter~\ref{chap:method}.

\section{The Cross-Domain Remote Sensing Evaluation Gap}
\label{sec:related-final}
% =============================================================================

A recurrent methodological limitation of the BCD literature is its reliance on
a small number of benchmark datasets that share similar acquisition conditions:
near-nadir viewing geometry, sub-meter to half-meter spatial resolution, binary
building-specific change labels, and predominantly planned or semi-planned urban
morphologies in East Asian metropolitan areas. LEVIR-CD \cite{Zheng2020LEVIR},
the de facto primary benchmark, consists of 637 image pairs from rapidly
developing Chinese cities acquired from a Google Earth composite with broadly
consistent radiometric properties across temporal epochs. DSIFN-CD
\cite{Zhang2020FusionNetDSIFN} provides heterogeneous multi-class change scenes
but remains within a nadir-view acquisition geometry. While these datasets have
been instrumental in establishing comparative performance baselines, they
constitute a narrow slice of the acquisition diversity encountered in real
operational deployments---a limitation that systematically biases the research
community's assessment of model robustness.

The WHU Building Change Detection Dataset \cite{Ji2019WHU} provides a partial
exception, offering centimetre-scale ($0.3\,\mathrm{m}$~px$^{-1}$) aerial
imagery over Christchurch, New Zealand---a city that underwent significant
reconstruction following seismic events---with high positional accuracy and
precisely delineated building annotations. The very high spatial resolution
and geometric accuracy of WHU-CD make it an important out-of-distribution
test for models trained on lower-resolution satellite imagery: features learned
from $0.5\,\mathrm{m}$ imagery may not transfer cleanly to the finer-grained
texture, shadow, and rooftop detail visible at $0.3\,\mathrm{m}$. LEVIR-CD+
\cite{LevirCDPlus} extends the original LEVIR-CD collection with additional
bi-temporal pairs covering a wider range of urban morphologies, including
high-density residential blocks, industrial zones, and peri-urban transitional
landscapes, providing a stress test for models whose training distribution may
not encompass the diversity of global urban form.

The SYSU-CD dataset \cite{ShiSYSU2022} introduces a further dimension of
evaluation difficulty by providing 20\,000 bi-temporal aerial image pairs
acquired over nine years in Guangzhou, China, with ground-truth labels that
encompass not only newly constructed buildings but also road alterations,
vegetation removal, and sea reclamation events. This multi-class annotation
scheme reflects the complexity of real urban change processes, in which
building construction is embedded within a broader matrix of simultaneous
environmental and infrastructure changes. Models trained on binary
building-only labels and evaluated on SYSU-CD must learn to suppress
non-building change signals---road resurfacing, seasonal vegetation cycles,
construction spoil---that would generate false alarms in a standard binary
formulation. The ability to maintain high precision under this condition is a
direct measure of the semantic specificity of the learned change representation,
a property that is not captured by any of the standard nadir-view binary
benchmarks.

The most challenging departure from standard evaluation conditions is
represented by the S2Looking dataset \cite{ShenS2Looking2021}, which consists
of 5\,000 bi-temporal image pairs acquired by side-looking satellites at
$0.5\,\mathrm{m}$ spatial resolution over multiple geographic regions
worldwide. The oblique acquisition geometry of side-looking sensors introduces
a family of systematic distortions that have no analogue in nadir-view imagery:
perspective foreshortening of building facades, layover effects in which tall
structures appear displaced toward the sensor, shadow geometry that depends on
both solar angle and viewing angle simultaneously, and apparent positional
shifts of rooftop boundaries between temporal acquisitions caused by differences
in the satellite's off-nadir viewing angle. These distortions mean that the
visual appearance of an unchanged building can differ substantially between two
side-looking acquisitions taken from slightly different orbital tracks,
generating strong false change signals in any model that relies on implicit
assumptions of geometric consistency between temporal pairs. Evaluating BCD
models on S2Looking thus tests a property distinct from all other benchmarks:
not the ability to detect structural change against a stable radiometric
background, but the ability to discriminate genuine change from the
geometry-induced apparent variation that is ubiquitous in oblique satellite
imagery.

The historical neglect of these diverse evaluation conditions has created a
systematic gap between the performance profiles reported in the BCD literature
and the generalization performance that would be observed in operational
deployments spanning multiple acquisition systems and global geographic regions.
Efficient attention variants, in particular, have been evaluated almost
exclusively on LEVIR-CD and DSIFN-CD, leaving entirely unknown whether the
rank-collapse pathologies identified for linear attention in dense prediction
benchmarks manifest more or less severely under the acquisition conditions
represented by WHU-CD, LEVIR-CD+, SYSU-CD, and S2Looking. The geometric
distortions of side-looking imagery, the class diversity of SYSU-CD, and the
centimetre-scale spatial detail of WHU-CD each impose different demands on the
attention mechanism: the former requires invariance to apparent positional
shifts, the second requires semantic selectivity, and the third requires the
preservation of fine-grained spatial detail that rank collapse would suppress.


The experimental program of this thesis closes this gap by subjecting the proposed RALA-ChangeFormer\allowbreak\ evaluation matrix spanning the full range of acquisition conditions described above. This matrix pairs the
foundational benchmarks---LEVIR-CD and DSIFN-CD---with four extended
datasets: WHU-CD, LEVIR-CD+, SYSU-CD, and S2Looking. Together they provide
a comprehensive empirical characterization of the accuracy-efficiency
trade-off profile of rank-augmented linear attention across heterogeneous
acquisition geometries, scene complexities, and annotation philosophies
encountered in global BCD practice. The results, analyzed in
Chapter~\ref{chap:experiments}, reveal the generalization boundaries of
rank-augmented attention and identify the specific acquisition
characteristics that govern whether the efficiency advantage can be
obtained without meaningful accuracy sacrifice.
